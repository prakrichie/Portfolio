Designing a guided first-week experience for new investors.

A Master's capstone project with Sharesies, reshaping how first-time investors learn, decide, and take their first step with confidence.

  • Overview

Overview

Sharesies helps everyday New Zealanders invest, but first-time users often froze before making their first move.

Over four months, I partnered with Sharesies to design a guided first-week experience that helps new investors understand their values, risk appetite, and goals, then turns that understanding into a confident first investment. The final prototype tested with eight new investors and carried forward three of five core features into the next design round.

The problem

New investors sign up ready to learn, but stall before their first trade. They're unsure what to buy, how much risk they're taking, or whether their choice matches their goals.

How might we

help new investors move from sign-up to their first confident investment in under a week?

Research

I ran interviews, co-design workshops, and a 38-response survey to understand where confidence breaks down.

Interviews

9 User Interviews

New and lapsed Sharesies users talked through their first week: where they got stuck, what they skipped, and what they wish they'd known.

Survey

38 User Findings

A broader read on confidence, goals, and risk literacy across first-year investors, validating the interview themes at scale.

Workshop

Workshop

A collaborative crazy 8's session with the Sharesies design team, combining the strongest sketches to craft key elements.

Solution

A four-part first-week experience that turns overwhelm into a clear path forward.

Step 01

A values-first questionnaire

Before any product is shown, users answer a short set of questions about what matters to them, how much risk they can sit with, and the goal behind investing. The answers become the spine of every recommendation that follows.

Questionnaire screen

Step 02

A personal journey map

A lightweight map shows where the user is in their first week, what's next, and why each step matters. It replaces the feeling of "what am I meant to do now?" with a visible path.

Journey map screen

Step 03

Learn content, exactly where it's needed

Short modules appear in the moments users need them, explaining risk as they set it, or investment types as they browse. Learning is embedded in the decision, not filed in a separate tab.

Learn module screen

Step 04

A confident first investment

The final step guides users through their first investment with a plain-language summary of what they're buying, why it matches their values, and what to expect next. A calm handoff from learning to doing.

First investment screen

Learnings

Two things I'll carry into every project after this.

Research drives every decision.

The features that tested best were the ones tied most directly to a specific insight from research. When I designed from assumption, the idea usually got cut. When I designed from a quote, it usually made it through.

Pivoting is a strength, not a setback.

Cutting and reworking features mid-prototype felt uncomfortable, but it sharpened the final experience. Holding ideas loosely, and being willing to kill them, is how the work gets better.
